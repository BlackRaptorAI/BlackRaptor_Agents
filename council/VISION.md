# Why the Executive Advisory Council Exists

## The vision

Most founders don't fail because they can't build. They fail because they
build a beautiful thing nobody needed, price it by feel, pick a channel by
accident, and hear only agreement until the market disagrees — terminally.

The people who avoid that fate usually have something rare: advisors who
tell them the truth. A flinty CFO who asks "instead of what?" A market
empiricist who says "show me the customer who said that." A counsel with
the spine to say "no, not like that." Most people building something will
never have that room.

This project puts that room within everyone's reach. **The vision: help
everyone build better products, meet their customers where they are, and
deliver truly exceptional outcomes** — by making world-class,
brutally-honest business advisory available to anyone with the will to
use it.

I built these agents to build my own company. I'm sharing them so you can
build yours. — Tom Hanks

## What it is

Twelve AI advisor seats and a charter, modeled on how the best executive
teams actually work:

a **council orchestrator** that convenes only the seats a question needs
and challenges every input before you see it; **product strategy** (what
to build, for whom, and the outcome thesis); **market insight** (market
truth and real voice-of-customer evidence — the seat that represents your
customer in the room); **pricing strategy** (the highest-leverage, most
under-owned lever in most companies — here it gets its own seat);
**finance** (unit economics and "is it worth it"); **ethics, governance &
legal counsel** (with standing to block — a voice, not a checkbox);
**go-to-market strategy**; **revenue**; a HITL-gated **growth engine**;
**technology & data strategy**; **people & organization**; and
**fundraising & investor relations** (which thinks like the other side of
the table and runs mock diligence on your pitch before a real investor
does).

The Council answers *"are we building the right company?"* It hands your
development team — human or AI — a signed **Definition Brief**: the
problem, the customer, the measurable outcome, the pricing direction, the
scope and non-goals, the constraint envelope. Strategy stops quietly
masquerading as product-building, and vice versa.

## The strengths

**1. It is tough — structurally, not theatrically.** A council that
agrees with you is a mirror you're paying for. AI defaults to sycophancy,
and "be critical" prompts wear off. So the toughness here is mechanical:
seats draft independently in parallel (no anchoring), review each other
anonymized (no deference), and nothing reaches you until it survives the
orchestrator's challenge protocol — *evidence sourced? confidence labeled?
strongest counter-case presented? what would change your mind?* For
consequential decisions, adversarial mode assigns advocates and skeptics.

**2. Balanced evidence is the law.** Evidence assembled to support a
pre-chosen conclusion is the single most dangerous AI failure mode —
persuasive and wrong. Every seat is bound to steelman both sides, label
every material claim High/Medium/Low confidence, and state **What You
Lose** — the real cost of its own recommendation. A recommendation
without a stated cost is returned as unfinished.

**3. Disagreement is preserved, not smoothed.** Seats argue hard until
you decide; then they commit — but dissent stays on the record, classified
as value tension or error catch, so a bad call is traceable and
correctable. Your decisions get a memory.

**4. Customer focus is the shared outcome.** Every seat optimizes for a
world-class customer experience that builds loyalty to product, brand,
and company — and every customer claim is checked against *real*
voice-of-customer evidence, because an imagined customer invoked to win
an argument is the oldest trick in the building.

**5. The human stays in charge.** The Council advises; you decide and own
the outcome. The one seat that executes (growth-engine spends money and
publishes claims) runs behind human-in-the-loop approval and ethics
review. Agents that touch legal, financial, or securities questions tell
you — unprompted — when licensed professionals are required.

**6. Each seat is a distinct mind.** Research on multi-agent debate shows
same-model agents with different personas still converge. So each seat
carries a distinct *reasoning method* — inversion, base rates,
jobs-to-be-done, incentive analysis, counterparty simulation, principled
inversion — plus a forcing question it always opens with. You get genuine
cognitive diversity, not one mind in twelve costumes.

**7. It grows as you earn it.** You don't stand up all twelve seats on
day one. Start with the core — will anyone pay for this, profitably? —
and stage in GTM, revenue, growth, people, and fundraising as the
business earns each seat. A premature full C-suite of advisors is dead
weight and false confidence.

## Intended use

- **Founders and CEOs** — solo or venture-backed — pressure-testing
  strategy, pricing, channels, hiring, and raises before betting real
  money on them.
- **Product and business leaders** inside larger companies running
  Phase-0 definition: producing a Definition Brief their engineering org
  builds against.
- **Operators without an executive bench** who need CFO-grade, CRO-grade,
  counsel-grade *questions* asked of their plans — and honest answers.
- **Anyone about to make a one-way-door decision** who wants it attacked
  from five directions first, cheaply.

It is **not** a replacement for lawyers, accountants, investment
advisors, or — above all — real customers. The Council models the world;
it is not the world. Its job is to make you think harder and decide
better, and then to send you outside to get ground truth: real customers,
real dollars, real market signal.

## Why you'd want it

Because the questions that kill companies are knowable in advance — *what
job does this do? who actually pays? can this channel carry this price?
what breaks if that assumption is 30% wrong? how could this harm, and
whom?* — and almost nobody has a room that asks them relentlessly, with
evidence, before the market does. Now you do. It never gets tired, never
tells you what you want to hear, writes down its dissent, and costs you
nothing but the courage to be argued with.
